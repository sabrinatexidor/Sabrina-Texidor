⚡ The Big Five — Pay Attention to These
Based on what we know about Gio's history, these are the areas most worth monitoring closely. Small shifts here can have a meaningful impact on seizure frequency.
Sleep Stress Levels Blood Sugar Sensory Overload Medication Consistency
🌿Daily Foundations
Simple daily habits that support the nervous system and reduce seizure triggers
Sleep
Possibly the Single Most Important Factor
Sleep deprivation is one of the most well-documented seizure triggers. Quality and consistency of sleep matters as much as quantity.
  • Blackout curtains — eliminate light disruption during sleep
  • No screens 60–90 minutes before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin and keeps the nervous system activated
  • Reduce sensory overload in the bedroom — sounds, lights, clutter all affect sleep quality
  • Consistent bedtime and wake time — predictability in sleep is key for seizure management
Blood Sugar Stability
Dips in Blood Sugar Can Be a Trigger
Keeping blood sugar stable throughout the day — through regular meals, limiting refined sugar and processed carbs, and not skipping meals — supports brain stability. This is also why the ketogenic diet (in the medical guide) has such strong evidence for epilepsy.
Caffeine & Alcohol
Both Are Known Nervous System Disruptors
Both caffeine and alcohol can lower the seizure threshold. Worth honestly assessing how much is in his daily routine — coffee, energy drinks, soda — and whether reducing them makes a difference.
Daily Physical Movement
Even a Walk Makes a Difference
Regular movement — even just a daily walk — reduces cortisol, improves sleep, and supports nervous system regulation. It doesn't need to be intense. Consistency matters more than intensity.
Hydration & Electrolytes
Simple and Often Overlooked
Dehydration can stress the nervous system. Adding electrolytes — especially sodium, potassium, and magnesium — supports proper nerve function.
Recommended: LMNT electrolytes — clean formula, no sugar, available online.

📊Tracking & Pattern Mapping
Map the 24–72 hours before a seizure to identify patterns and triggers
The 24–72 Hour Map
One of the Most Valuable Things the Family Can Do
After each seizure, look back at the 24–72 hours before it and ask: what changed? This isn't about blame — it's about pattern recognition. Over time, themes often emerge that can help predict and prevent.
  • How was his sleep the night before? Two nights before?
  • Did he eat differently — skip a meal, have more sugar, less protein?
  • Was stress unusually high? A conflict, a change in routine, an overwhelm?
  • Was he around more sensory stimulation than usual — loud environments, bright lights, crowds?
  • Did he miss or take his medication at a different time?
  • Was there more screen time than usual?
  • Had he been drinking less water?
Keep a simple notes app or journal entry after each seizure. Even a few bullet points builds a picture over time.
Predictable Daily Schedule
What He Can Control Matters
A predictable daily rhythm — consistent wake time, meals, movement, wind-down — gives the nervous system a sense of safety and reduces the kind of dysregulation that can precede a seizure. Predictability is medicine.

💊Supplements Worth Exploring
Always review with his neurologist first — some supplements interact with anti-seizure medications
⚠️ Important
Some supplements — especially CBD and high-dose Omega-3 — can interact with anti-seizure medications. Review all of these with his neurologist before starting.
Magnesium L-Threonate
The Most Neurologically Active Form of Magnesium
Magnesium plays a critical role in nerve function and brain excitability. L-Threonate is the form that crosses the blood-brain barrier most effectively, making it the most relevant for neurological support. Many people with epilepsy are deficient in magnesium.
Review with neurologistWell-tolerated by most
Omega-3 Fatty Acids
Anti-Inflammatory, Brain-Supportive
Omega-3s support brain health and have anti-inflammatory properties. Some research suggests they may reduce seizure frequency. Fish oil or algae-based Omega-3 are both good sources.
Review with neurologist
Vitamin D
Deficiency Is Common & Worth Testing
Vitamin D deficiency is common and associated with increased seizure activity. A simple blood test can check his levels. Supplementing if deficient is low-risk and potentially meaningful.
Ask neurologist to test levels
CBD (Cannabidiol)
FDA-Approved Form Exists — Worth a Conversation
CBD has well-documented anti-seizure properties — in fact, an FDA-approved CBD medication called Epidiolex exists specifically for drug-resistant epilepsy. Over-the-counter CBD products vary widely in quality. This is one to have an explicit conversation with his neurologist about, not to start independently.
Ask neurologist about EpidiolexOTC products vary in quality
Melatonin
For Sleep Quality — Low Dose, Consistent Timing
Melatonin can help regulate sleep cycles, particularly if his sleep onset is inconsistent. Low doses (0.5–1mg) taken 30–60 minutes before bed are most effective. Higher doses aren't necessarily better.
Generally safe — review with doctor
Ketogenic Diet & Keto Chow
High Fat, Moderate Protein, Low Carb — Strong Evidence for Epilepsy
The ketogenic diet changes how the brain uses fuel — instead of glucose, it runs on ketones, which have a stabilizing effect on brain activity. It has over 100 years of clinical use for epilepsy and strong evidence for drug-resistant cases. Studies show more than 50% of people who try it see a meaningful reduction in seizures.
As a starting point for exploring how this way of eating feels, Keto Chow shakes are a delicious, nutritionally complete meal replacement that fits a ketogenic macronutrient profile — a low-barrier way to experiment with how the body responds to ketones before committing to the full dietary therapy.
For the full medical version: Yale and Boston Children's Hospital both have dedicated adult ketogenic diet programs supervised by a neurologist and dietitian. See the medical guide for details.
The full ketogenic diet as a medical therapy is not a DIY approach — it requires medical supervision. But exploring keto-friendly eating as a starting point is low risk and worth trying.
Discuss full keto with neurologist Keto Chow available online

🧘Nervous System & Stress
Stress regulation is major — possibly the most underestimated factor in seizure management
Stress Is Major
The Nervous System Needs Tools — Not Just Awareness
When Gio is stressed, his nervous system needs go-to practices to regulate — not just the awareness that he's stressed, but actual tools he can use in the moment. The goal is to move from fight/flight activation back toward a settled state. These don't need to be elaborate.
Body Scan
A Simple, Powerful Regulation Tool
A body scan is simply the practice of slowly moving attention through different parts of the body — noticing how each part feels without trying to change anything. This brings the nervous system out of its head and into the present moment. It can be done in 3 minutes lying down or sitting.
Apps like Insight Timer have free guided body scans. This is a good starting point.
Orienting to the Room
A Somatic Grounding Technique
When the nervous system is activated, slowly looking around the room and noticing specific details — what's on the wall, the colors, the textures, the smells — signals to the brain that the environment is safe. This is a technique from somatic therapy and it works precisely because it's so simple.
Understanding the Flight/Fight Response
His Nervous System Has Been in High Alert Since Childhood
Gio's seizures began in childhood — which means his nervous system has been navigating this for his entire developmental period. As a result, his fight/flight response may be chronically activated, even subtly. It can also be hard to trust your own body when it has felt unpredictable for years. This is not a character flaw — it's a physiological adaptation. Nervous system work is about gradually building a felt sense of safety.

💬Therapy & Emotional Support
Processing the emotional and psychological impact of living with epilepsy since childhood
Why Therapy?
This Isn't About Something Being Wrong — It's About What He's Been Through
For many people with chronic conditions that began in childhood, there is a psychological and nervous system dimension that deserves its own attention. Gio has been navigating seizures his entire life — which shapes identity, sense of control, trust in the body, and family dynamics in ways that aren't always visible. Therapy isn't about fixing something broken. It's about having a space to process all of it.
Key themes worth exploring: sense of safety, trust in his own body, self-regulation, medical trauma, and the emotional impact on the family system.
Trauma-Informed CBT
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy with a Trauma Lens
Traditional CBT focuses on the relationship between thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. Trauma-informed CBT adapts this approach to account for how trauma and chronic stress shape those patterns — particularly relevant for someone whose nervous system has been impacted from early childhood.
Somatic Therapy — EMDR, ART, Brainspotting, Neurofeedback
Body-Based Approaches to Trauma Processing
Somatic therapies work directly with the body's stored stress responses — not just the mind. These are particularly effective for people whose nervous systems have been chronically activated.
  • EMDR — Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing. Widely used for trauma, helps the brain process stuck experiences.
  • ART (Accelerated Resolution Therapy) — uses eye movements and imagery to reprocess distressing memories quickly.
  • Brainspotting — uses eye position to access and process trauma stored in the subcortical brain.
  • Neurofeedback — trains the brain to regulate its own electrical activity. Has specific evidence for epilepsy as well as trauma.
ACT — Acceptance and Commitment Therapy
Learning to Live Fully Alongside a Chronic Condition
ACT is particularly well-suited for chronic conditions. Rather than fighting or trying to eliminate difficult experiences, it focuses on building psychological flexibility — the ability to move toward what matters even when things are hard. Very relevant for someone managing epilepsy long-term.
Support Group
Meeting People Who Actually Understand
There is something uniquely valuable about honest, vulnerable conversations with people who share the same lived experience. A seizure-specific support group — in person or online — can reduce isolation and provide a kind of understanding that even the most loving family can't fully provide.
Find one: Epilepsy Foundation CT hosts support groups and can connect Gio to others in his area.

💍Tracking Devices
Wearables that track sleep, stress, and recovery alongside seizure monitoring
Oura Ring
Tracks Sleep, HRV, Stress & Recovery — FSA/HSA Eligible
The Oura Ring is a wellness tracking ring worn on the finger that monitors sleep quality, heart rate variability (HRV), body temperature, activity, and recovery. Several people with epilepsy have found it valuable — both for tracking patterns and for understanding how stress and sleep affect their overall state. It comes in silver, gold, and black.
While not a medical seizure detector, it can be a powerful tool for the 24–72 hour pattern mapping — helping identify nights of poor sleep, elevated stress signals, or unusual recovery patterns before a seizure.
